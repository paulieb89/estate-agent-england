# England Residential Estate Agent Compliance Reference

Practical compliance obligations for England residential estate agency work. This is a reference
summary, not legal advice. Consult a solicitor or compliance specialist for definitive guidance.

---

## 1. Redress Scheme Membership

**Legal requirement** — all residential estate agents in England must belong to an approved
consumer redress scheme before carrying on estate agency activity.

**Governing legislation:** The Redress Schemes for Lettings Agency Work and Property Management Work
(Requirement to Belong to a Scheme etc) (England) Order 2014; Estate Agents Act 1979 (as amended).

**Approved schemes (residential sales):**
- The Property Ombudsman (TPO) — tpos.co.uk
- Property Redress Scheme (PRS) — theprs.co.uk

**Failure to belong:** Criminal offence. Maximum fine: £5,000 per instance. Trading Standards enforce.

**Practical implication:** Membership must be in place before the first instruction is taken. The
redress scheme details must be disclosed to consumers. Membership number should appear on all
marketing and correspondence.

## 2. HMRC Anti-Money Laundering Supervision

**Legal requirement** — estate agency businesses must register with HMRC for money laundering
supervision before carrying on estate agency activity.

**Governing legislation:** Money Laundering, Terrorist Financing and Transfer of Funds (Information
on the Payer) Regulations 2017 (as amended, including the 2019 and 2022 amendments).

**Who must register:** Any business that "carries out estate agency work" as defined in s.1 of the
Estate Agents Act 1979. This includes introducing buyers to sellers of residential and commercial
property.

**HMRC supervision register:** searchable at gov.uk (HMRC AML register).

**Failure to register:** Criminal offence. Can result in prosecution, fines, and business closure.

**Customer Due Diligence (CDD):**
- Required on clients (sellers) at instruction and on buyers making a successful offer
- Enhanced due diligence required for high-risk customers, PEPs, and high-value transactions
- Source of funds checks required before exchange on transactions above certain thresholds
- CDD records must be kept for 5 years after the business relationship ends

**Plugin boundary:** This plugin does not make AML/KYC decisions. All AML matters must be handled
by the responsible agent's MLRO. Do not interpret plugin output as AML clearance.

## 3. Estate Agents Act 1979 (EAA1979)

**Key provisions relevant to residential sales:**

**s.1 — Definition of estate agency work:** The scope of the Act. Applies to introducing parties
to transactions involving land in the UK.

**s.18 — Disclosure of connected services:** An agent must disclose in writing to clients any
financial benefit, fee, or commission that the agent or a connected person may receive from services
provided to either party (e.g., mortgage referral, conveyancing referral, survey panel fees).

**s.21 — Personal interest:** An agent who has a personal interest in a property must disclose
this in writing to any prospective buyer and to the client before accepting instructions.

**s.3 and s.4 — Undesirable practices:** The OFT/CMA can apply to ban agents who have engaged in
specified undesirable practices, including: making false or misleading statements about properties,
discrimination, failure to pass on offers, failing to keep clients informed.

**Misleading descriptions:** Misrepresentation in property particulars can give rise to liability
under the Misrepresentation Act 1967, the Consumer Protection from Unfair Trading Regulations 2008
(now superseded by DMCCA in B2C contexts), and under the common law of deceit/negligent misstatement.

## 4. Consumer Protection / DMCCA 2024

**Governing legislation:** Digital Markets, Competition and Consumers Act 2024 (DMCCA), which
replaced the Consumer Protection from Unfair Trading Regulations 2008 (CPUTR) as the primary B2C
unfair commercial practices regime.

**Core prohibition:** Businesses must not engage in unfair commercial practices including:
- Misleading actions (false or deceptive statements, including omissions)
- Misleading omissions (withholding information a buyer needs to make an informed transactional
  decision)
- Aggressive commercial practices (pressure selling, harassment)

**Key test — misleading omission:** Information is "material" if an average consumer needs it to
make an informed decision about whether to make an offer, view a property, or proceed to exchange.
This is a contextual, principle-based test — not a fixed checklist.

**Enforcement:** Trading Standards / Competition and Markets Authority (CMA). CMA has direct
enforcement powers under DMCCA including civil and criminal routes.

**Practical implication for listings:** Agents should disclose all information that could affect a
buyer's decision, including known adverse factors. Burying or omitting significant adverse information
(e.g., short lease, cladding issues, flood history) risks a DMCCA breach.

## 5. Material Information — Post-NTS Position (as at 2025)

**Background:** The National Trading Standards Estate and Letting Agents Team (NTS) published
material-information guidance in three parts (A, B, C) under the CPUTR 2008 regime. This guidance
has been formally withdrawn following DMCCA 2024 replacing CPUTR.

**Current position:**
- NTS Parts A, B, C are withdrawn and should not be cited as definitive law
- MHCLG consulted in 2024–2025 on producing government-issued statutory guidance for residential
  sales material information under DMCCA
- At the time this reference was prepared, no replacement statutory guidance had been finalised

**Practical approach:** Apply the principle-based DMCCA test: disclose everything a typical buyer
would need to make an informed decision about this specific property. Use the material-information-
checklist as a working standard, noting it is not legally definitive.

**Design note:** This plugin is intentionally config-driven, not hard-coded to a specific NTS
checklist version. The material-information-checklist.md reference file should be updated as
government guidance evolves.

## 6. TPO / PRS Code Expectations

**The Property Ombudsman Residential Estate Agents Code** (and the equivalent Property Redress
Scheme code) sets the expected standards of conduct for members. While not statute, breaches
can lead to compensation awards, disciplinary action, and reputational damage.

**Key behavioural expectations:**
- Provide honest, accurate, and not misleading information about properties
- Disclose all known material facts that may affect a buyer's decision
- Not apply undue pressure on buyers or sellers
- Pass on all offers to the seller promptly and accurately
- Keep clients informed throughout the transaction
- Handle complaints promptly and fairly

**Description accuracy:** Particulars must not include any inaccuracy, misrepresentation, or
misleading omission. Known defects or issues must be disclosed.

**Conflicts of interest:** Must be disclosed in writing.

## 7. No Legal Advice / Human Review Boundaries

Estate agents are not solicitors and cannot give legal advice.

**Always refer to a solicitor or conveyancer:**
- Title, easements, covenants, and rights of way
- Boundary disputes
- Defective title or title gaps
- Planning enforcement, breaches, and appeals
- Building regulations compliance for past works

**Always refer to a surveyor or specialist:**
- Structural condition, damp, or subsidence
- Japanese knotweed identification and treatment
- Cladding and fire safety assessments (EWS1)
- Flood risk surveys

**Always refer to the MLRO (Money Laundering Reporting Officer):**
- Suspicious transaction reports
- CDD or enhanced due diligence decisions
- PEP/sanction screening results

**This plugin:** Does not give legal advice. Does not make AML decisions. Does not constitute a
compliance sign-off. All outputs require human review by the responsible agent before use.
